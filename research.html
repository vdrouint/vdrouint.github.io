Fractional vortices and confinement

Astonishing physics sometimes reveals itself in the most unlikely places. The melting of 54COOBC,a molecular liquid crystal film of a single layer, exhibits a phase with a hidden order parameter. In order to explain the unexpected specific heat signal and the peculiar sequence of phase transitions, I studied the finite temperature phase diagram of a system of coupled classical vectors. The results, obtained using a specially devised Monte-Carlo algorithm, show the existence of a new phase which was not previously found. The nematic-hexatic model studied encompasses the competition of nematic molecular order and hexatic bond-orientational order; the challenge here lies in the competition between the two global symmetries. These end up in a state of relative three-state Potts locking.

The hidden order phase involves a coexistence of free vortices of unit charge with long-range discrete Potts order. The unit charge vortices are bound states of three fractional vortices, and resemble baryons formed from a bound state of three confined quarks. Thus, the solution to this long-standing mystery phase in the liquid crystals is a classical analog of quark confinement.

Our preprint is available on the arXiv, or here. I am currently exploring whether dual representations of this model can further reveal the relative order phase.

Iron-Based Superconductors

The iron-based superconductors are multiorbital systems, in which each iron atom is contained within a tetrahedral environment. The Hund's coupling tends to align the spins in the different orbitals, and this is an important driver of strong electron correlations.

I have developed a many-body treatment of the Hund-Kondo model, in which the iron atom is viewed as an impurity in the metallic environment. Using slave-fermion methods, this leads to a set of self-consistent integral equations for the self energies of holons and spinons which describe the valence and spin fluctuations of the iron atom.

The first publication stemming from this work was published in PRB where we studied the three-orbital Hund-Kondo model using large-N Schwinger Bosons. A follow-up of this work which includes valence fluctuations is in preparation. We are now extending the approach to include spin-orbit coupling at the impurity, which can lead to local pairing of the electrons.
